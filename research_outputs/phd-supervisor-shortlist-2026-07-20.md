# Prospective Supervisors & Host Labs — ranked shortlist

**Date:** 2026-07-20 · **Companion:** `phd-proposal-2026-07-20.md`
**The core insight:** this thesis sits at the **intersection of two fields that rarely overlap** — (a) **precision µN/nN force metrology** (torsion balances) and (b) **high-Q / SRF resonant cavities**. The ideal host has *both*, or a co-supervision spanning them. That framing drives the ranking.

---

## Tier 1 — the natural home (has BOTH capabilities, already tests this device class)

### 1. Prof. Martin Tajmar — Chair of Space Systems / "Breakthrough Propulsion Physics", TU Dresden (Germany) ⭐ top choice
- **Why #1:** the *only* group in the world purpose-built for exactly this — a **100 nN (→ ~5 nN) automated torsion balance with laser interferometry**, in-vacuum, already used to **null-test the EmDrive and Mach-Effect thrusters**, with a superconducting-levitation balance line too. They own the apparatus, the systematics expertise (thermal/EM), and the publication track record.
- **Fit to the proposal:** the Q-scaling discriminator + orthogonal stack is a *natural, fundable extension* of their existing programme — a methodological upgrade to work they already do, not a cold start.
- **Approach:** lead with the **methods contribution** (the Q-ladder discriminator), cite their nulls as the state of the art you build on, and attach the reproducible package. This is the single highest-probability path to a real, credible build.
- **Realistic caveat:** funding for breakthrough-propulsion is tight and competitive; a co-funded or externally-scholarshipped student is more attractive.

## Tier 2 — precision-thrust-metrology groups (have the balance; would need a cavity/SRF partner)

### 2. Prof. Michael Keidar group (Kolbeck/Porter) — The George Washington University (USA)
- **Why:** published **high-precision thrust-balance development**; strong electric-propulsion µN metrology. US-based, English-language, active grad program.
- **Gap:** electric-propulsion focus, not resonant cavities — would need to pair with an SRF/RF group. Good for the *metrology* half.

### 3. Purdue University — High Vacuum Lab (electric propulsion)
- **Why:** operational **electrostatic-fin-calibrated thrust stand** (<3% repeatability at >50 µN), strong vacuum + propulsion infrastructure.
- **Gap:** same as GWU — propulsion metrology, not high-Q cavities.

### 4. Institute of Mechanics, CAS (China) · National University of Defense Technology (China)
- **Why:** state-of-the-art **sub-0.03 µN torsion balances, electrostatic-comb calibration** — technically among the best. Listed for completeness of the metrology landscape.
- **Gap:** access/collaboration practicality for an external student; not a realistic first approach from Canada.

## Tier 3 — the SRF/cryo half (Canada-accessible; the Q-ladder partner)

### 5. TRIUMF SRF facility (Vancouver) + University of Victoria / Western University (Canada) ⭐ best Canadian route
- **Why:** **Canada's only SRF centre** — cavity test cryostats, Q₀ metrology, Nb/Nb₃Sn processing, and a **graduate/postgraduate SRF training program** with university partners **UVic** and **Western (UWO)**, NSERC-funded. This is exactly the **cryo-copper → Nb₃Sn Q-ladder** half of the experiment, *in your country*.
- **Fit:** ideal **co-host / collaboration** for the high-Q rung and the cavity fabrication/measurement. Pair a physics-department supervisor (UVic/Western) for the degree with TRIUMF for the SRF infrastructure.
- **Caveat:** TRIUMF is an accelerator lab — they'd engage on the *cavity/Q* science, and would (reasonably) want the "propulsion" framing kept rigorously as a precision-measurement / novel-cavity-diagnostics project.

## Tier 4 — adjacent precision-force groups (fallback metrology expertise)
- **Gravitational / short-range-force torsion-balance groups** (e.g. Eöt-Wash-style precision-gravity labs) — world-class at nN torsion metrology and systematics, though not propulsion-focused. A possible metrology co-supervisor if the primary host lacks balance depth.

## Recommended approach (sequenced)
1. **Lead with Tajmar (TU Dresden).** Highest technical fit by far; the proposal is a direct extension of their programme. One concise email: the methods contribution (Q-ladder discriminator) + the reproducible package + a request to discuss a co-supervised/scholarshipped PhD.
2. **In parallel, open the Canadian route:** a physics supervisor at **UVic or Western** co-supervising with **TRIUMF** for the SRF/cryo Q-ladder — geographically practical from Toronto, and strong for the high-Q half.
3. **Hold GWU/Purdue as metrology-half fallbacks** if a cavity/SRF partner can be attached.

## Positioning rules (for every outreach)
- **Frame it as precision metrology + a novel discriminator**, never "anti-gravity." (A committee funds "the tightest bound + a reusable methodology," not "a working drive.")
- **Cite the Dresden nulls as the state of the art you extend** — this signals you know the field and aren't a crank.
- **Attach the reproducible package** (19-check harness, artifact budget, subsystem optimization) — it demonstrates seriousness and does the credibility work up front.
- **Keep the extraordinary-claim posture:** the value is a *credible answer either way*.

*Contact details and current-year confirmation of each group's activity should be verified before outreach; personnel and funding shift year-to-year. This shortlist is a starting map, not a vetted contact list.*
